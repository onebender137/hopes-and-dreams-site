Beneficial and adverse health effects of kratom (Mitragyna speciosa): A critical review of the literature

Abstract
Used in Southeast Asia for generations, kratom gained popularity in the United States and elsewhere over the past several decades. Derived from Mitragyna speciosa, kratom preparations including leaves, teas, powders, capsules, and extracts may yield stimulant, analgesic, and opioid-like effects that occur dose-dependently based on concentrations of kratom's key alkaloids, mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine. Such effects are responsible for kratom's potential as a reduced-harm alternative to opiates and as a withdrawal treatment. But these properties are also associated with tolerance development and addictive potential. Given mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine activity on cytochrome P450 isoforms and opioid receptors, adverse effects among polysubstance users are a concern. Current literature on the toxicology of kratom is reviewed, including product alkaloid concentrations, in vitro and in vivo data, epidemiological evidence, and human case data. The potential harms and benefits of kratom products are discussed within an exposure assessment framework, and recommendations for industry are presented. Current evidence indicates that kratom may have therapeutic potential in some persons and that products present few risks with typical, non-polysubstance use. However, few studies identified alkaloid doses at which adverse effects were expected in humans or animals. Such research is needed to inform future assessments of kratom's risks and benefits.
Introduction
Kratom, the common term for leaves or other products derived from the Southeast Asian tree Mitragyna speciosa, was first commercially introduced into the United States in the 1980s and has gained popularity since the mid-2000s (Henningfield et al., 2018; Kruegel and Grundmann, 2018; Veltri and Grundmann, 2019). It has been used for hundreds of years in Southeast Asia both recreationally and medicinally, largely due to the properties of its alkaloids mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine (Eastlack et al., 2020; Grundmann, 2017; Singh et al., 2017). Depending on the country, kratom is referred to by its local name: ketum or biak (Malaysia), keton or kadamba (Indonesia), benisa (Myanmar), mambog or lugub (Philippines), ithang, thom, kratom (Thailand), or giam (Vietnam) (Cinosi et al., 2015; Eastlack et al., 2020; Gong et al., 2012; Grundmann, 2017).
Mitragynine (CAS #4098-40-2), the most significant kratom alkaloid, has a molecular weight of 398.5 g/mol; melting and boiling points of 104 and 235 °C, respectively; experimental octanol:water partition coefficient (logP) of 1.7; log ionization constant (pKa) of 8.1; and empirical formula, which is shared with numerous stereoisomers, of C23H30N2O4 (Amrianto et al., 2021; Ramanathan et al., 2015). The structures of mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine can be seen in Fig. 1.
Kratom was historically used in rural communities by farmers, laborers, or fishermen as a stimulant to reduce or prevent fatigue stemming from hard labor by chewing the leaves raw or drying them for later consumption, which then occurred via teas or chewing (Cinosi et al., 2015; Grundmann, 2017; Singh et al., 2017). Some of these effects are anecdotal. Though not a hallucinogen, kratom's association with a sense of euphoria also motivated early users, as did offering it as a gift to gods or deceased ancestors (Saingam et al., 2016; Swogger et al., 2015).
Traditionally consumed as a tea or dry leaves, more recent commercialized kratom products include powders, capsules, and liquid extracts (Singh et al., 2016). Medicinally, kratom has, over the years, been used to treat symptoms of substance abuse disorders (including opioid withdrawal), headaches, toothaches, diarrhea, cough, fever, stroke symptoms, sleeplessness, typhoid, cholesterol issues, pain, cough, hypertension, diabetes, anxiety, and as a orexigenic, wound poultice, and deworming agent (Ahmad et al., 2022; Eastlack et al., 2020; Gong et al., 2012; Grundmann, 2017; Singh et al., 2017). It is not uncommon for herbals to be considered almost a cure-all for a myriad of ailments, although few of the alleged benefits have been studied in controlled settings. In the United States, kratom is sold primarily at vape shops or direct to consumers via online commerce (Bowdring et al., 2023; Williams and Nikitin, 2020).
There are currently no estimates for global prevalence of kratom use due to the variance in data availability and tracking across countries (Bergen-Cico and MacClurg, 2016). In Thailand, for example, it was reported that lifetime prevalence of kratom use was 2.3% for those aged 12–65 and past year use was 0.8% (Assanangkornchai et al., 2007). In the United States, it is estimated that up to 10–16 million people use kratom (Swogger et al., 2022). That figure may be rising. Various authors and health agencies reported that national overall kratom use prevalence ranged from 1.3% to 6.1% and prevalence of past-year use ranged from 0.6% to 0.8%, as of 2019 and 2021, respectively, in the United States (Palamar, 2021; Schimmel et al., 2021; Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration, 2022; Swogger et al., 2022).
The most frequently reported reasons for kratom use in the U.S. include self-treatment for pain, fatigue, coughing, diarrhea, anxiety and depression, and opioid withdrawal (Drug Enforcement Administration, 2020; Food and Drug Administration (FDA), 2024; National Institute on Drug Abuse, 2022; Rogers et al., 2021). Significantly, such uses have not been evaluated by the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) or rigorously studied by kratom processors and distributors. The FDA has not approved any prescription or over-the-counter products containing kratom, mitragynine, or 7-hydroxymitragynine for market in the U.S. (U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), 2023). As of early 2024, the Administration classified kratom as:
“a new dietary ingredient for which there is inadequate information to provide reasonable assurance that such ingredient does not present a significant or unreasonable risk of illness or injury and, therefore, dietary supplements that are or contain kratom are adulterated under section 402(f)(1)(B) of the Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FD&C)" (U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), 2023).
The United States Drug Enforcement Agency (DEA) classifies kratom as a drug of concern (United States Drug Enforcement Agency (DEA), 2024). Previously, the DEA moved to classify kratom as a Schedule I compound under the Controlled Substances Act (CSA) in 2016 following FDA product seizures (Commons, 2018; Corkery et al., 2019; Henningfield et al., 2018; U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration, 2016). The DEA's proposed regulation was never implemented following stakeholder response that included 1,000s of comment submissions from consumers, many of which noted that kratom use had positively affected commenters' lives through easing withdrawals and other issues (Corkery et al., 2019; Henningfield et al., 2018).
In 2021, the World Health Organization's (WHO) Expert Committee on Drug Dependence (ECDD) examined the evidence regarding kratom's health effects and “… concluded that there is insufficient evidence of adverse effects that would warrant additional critical review or inclusion in the United Nations list of internationally controlled substances” (National Institute on Drug Abuse, 2023). Currently, kratom remains on the list of substances under surveillance by the WHO ECDD (National Institute on Drug Abuse, 2023).
The purpose of this paper is to examine the current uses of kratom, the alleged benefits, the health risks associated with various alkaloid doses or estimated doses, the regulatory safeguards (or lack thereof), and needs for additional research. Our evaluation should be helpful to users, physicians who care for or treat persons who use this substance, lawyers involved in cases associated with kratom, and various global regulatory bodies that may choose to regulate it.
Kratom's increasing popularity in the U.S., along with rapidly evolving evidence concerning the plant's potential pharmacologic benefits and toxicological profile, warrant further exploration. Here, we review the literature on the pharmacokinetics and toxicology (in vitro, in vivo, and human) of kratom, mitragynine, and 7-hydroxymitragyine. Our work is more inclusive than other recent reviews, which either overlooked historic or recent in vivo or in vitro data, examined only pharmacokinetic parameters, or reported on a small selection of effects and not a wider suite of toxic endpoints (Eastlack et al., 2020; Firmansyah et al., 2021; Kerrigan and Basiliere, 2021; Leong Bin Abdullah and Singh, 2021; Prevete et al., 2023; Schimmel and Dart, 2020; Ya et al., 2019).
Toxicologically, various alkaloids contribute to the therapeutic and other effects of kratom, most notably mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine. Firmansyah et al. (2021) reported that six of the alkaloids in kratom have psychoactive properties, including mitragynine, 7-hydroxymitragynine, mitraphylline, speciociliatine, speciogynine, and paynantheine. Similar to alkaloids derived from the Coffea species (i.e., coffee) and other members of the Rubiaceae, kratom-derived alkaloids have dose-dependent stimulant properties (Ahmad et al., 2022).
Over 50 alkaloids have been reported in M. speciosa (Brown et al., 2017). Of these, mitragynine is the most prominent in plant materials and marketed products (Henningfield et al., 2018, 2022b). Specifically, mitragynine comprises approximately 2% of the dry weight of kratom leaves, and approximately 12–66% of the total alkaloid content of the leaves, depending on plant origin (Brown et al., 2017; Firmansyah et al., 2021). In contrast, 7-hydroxymitragynine generally comprises less than 0.02% of the dry weight and less than 2% of the alkaloid content of dry kratom leaves (Brown et al., 2017; Firmansyah et al., 2021; Ponglux et al., 1994). Seasonality of harvest, relative humidity, water status, soil calcium and magnesium, light levels, and general geography are known to influence the alkaloid content of kratom leaves (Boffa et al., 2018; Leksungnoen et al., 2022; Sengnon et al., 2023; Shellard et al., 1978; Zhang et al., 2020). The relationship between mitragynine and 7-hydroxymitragynine is discussed later.
Various authors have reported the alkaloid content of commercially significant kratom products. Fowble and Musah (2019) reported mitragynine at 2.76–14.02 mg/g in dried kratom plant materials, 3.16–13.66 mg/g in powdered products, and 16.65–20.05 mg/g in capsule products. Sharma et al. (2019) reported that, by weight, various marketed capsule products contained 4.71–38.74% mitragynine, 0.01–0.04% 7-hydroxymitragynine, 1.36–12.29% speciociliatine, 0.87–5.29% speciogynine, 0.08–0.88% corynantheidine, 0.05–0.13% isocorynantheidine, and 0.03–0.43% corynoxine. The same authors reported that lab-formulated lyophilized kratom tea contained 0.74% mitragynine and 0.01% 7-hydroxymitragynine by weight (Sharma et al., 2019). Others reported that mitragynine concentrations in capsule products purchased in the Chicago, Illinois, area in 2019 ranged from 3.35 to 60.36 mg/g, while powdered products contained 9.34–10.12 mg/g (Prozialeck et al., 2020). Products marketed as liquid kratom extracts contain up to approximately 118 mg of mitragynine per 8 ml container (Fleming et al., 2023). Liquid extracts generally contain <0.02 mg 7-hydroxymitragynine per container to <0.005% by weight (Fleming et al., 2023). As noted above, there is evidence that kratom and its derivative products may yield pharmacologic benefits at certain doses in some users. These include effects as a stimulant, analgesic, and as a means of alleviating opioid withdrawal symptoms (Hassan et al., 2013; Prozialeck et al., 2012; Singh et al., 2016; Swogger et al., 2022; Warner et al., 2016). At the same time, kratom and mitragynine have been cited as factors in several studies of overdose deaths across the U.S. (Gershman et al., 2019; Olsen et al., 2019). However, morbidity and mortality associated with kratom and its alkaloids is confounded in these case reports because many of the users take other agents. For example, it is not uncommon in these cases for the patient to have fairly high concentrations of prescription, non-prescription, and illicit drugs, along with alcohol, in their blood, which is reported to increase the odds of adverse outcomes secondary to kratom use (Henningfield et al., 2019; Post et al., 2019).
Henningfield et al. (2019) reported that, given the lack of clear association between mitragynine blood levels and physiologic effects in humans, using mitragynine as an indicator of kratom mortality was of unclear validity. At the very least, it is often quite difficult to attribute a specific adverse effect or death to any singular agent, especially kratom. Given its historical uses, it is unlikely that kratom or mitragynine, alone, were responsible for the vast majority of deaths in which kratom alkaloids were detected in the blood of decedents.
We performed a non-systematic, critical review of the literature to examine reported health effects with use of or exposure to kratom and its alkaloids. For purposes of our review, online tools (PubMed, Google Scholar) were queried for various combinations of terms including “kratom,” “Mitragyna speciosa,” “mitragynine,” “toxic*,” “in vitro,” “in vivo,” “animal,” “pharmacokinetic(s),” “case,” “epidemiolog*,” and various other relevant alkaloids (e.g., “corynantheidine,” “speciociliatine”), with results current through February 2024. The references cited in various published papers were examined to identify additional publications of merit. Non-English studies were excluded but given the comparatively narrow focus of this body of literature, no other a priori inclusion or exclusion criteria were applied. Approximately 600 references including peer-reviewed publications, book chapters, government guidelines, and kratom processor and distributor documents were reviewed in our initial search. From this, we discuss approximately 200 of the most informative resources in the below narrative. The goal of our search was to identify and contextualize the chronology and scope of extant published literature on the pharmacology and toxicology of kratom and its most significant alkaloids to better inform future human health risk assessments and improve industry best practices.

Summary and conclusions
Recent literature concerning the pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, pharmacology, and toxicology of kratom mixtures, mitragynine, 7-hydroxymitragynine, and several other associated alkaloids was reviewed here. Traditional kratom consumption via teas or chewing of dry leaves, where mitragynine concentrations are generally <15–30 mg/g and doses often <2–20 mg/kg-bw/day, is not expected to yield adverse health effects in human users.
